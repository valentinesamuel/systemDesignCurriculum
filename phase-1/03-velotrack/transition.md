# Leaving VeloTrack

Ten months at VeloTrack. The Kafka architecture is solid. The delivery state machine
has been running without a single ghost delivery for six months — SupremeFreight
renewed their contract with a 30% expansion. Emeka gives you a referral bonus even
though you don't ask for one. "You saved SupremeFreight," he says. "That's $3M ARR."

The real-time tracking system went live two months after you designed it: 500,000
active drivers, 200,000 concurrent WebSocket connections at peak, P99 latency at
1.8 seconds. Not perfect, but within SLA. The team calls it "the best thing we've
shipped."

You leave on good terms. Emeka asks if you'd consider staying; he's offering a lead
role. You tell him honestly: "I think I need to work somewhere that's been around
longer and broken more things. I want to see what happens after the startup phase."

He nods. He gets it.

**Slack DM — Marcus Webb → You, your last day**

**Marcus Webb**
Logistics. Kafka. State machines. Three solid additions to your mental model.
Here's what I'd work on next: media, storage, and content delivery. Everything
you've done so far is transactional — small, fast, consistent writes.
Storage and streaming are the other half of the world.
Find somewhere that deals with large binary data at scale.

**Two weeks later**

You get an introduction through a former colleague to **Beacon Media** — a streaming
content platform serving 28 million monthly active users across Asia and Africa.
Think of them as a regional Netflix: video on demand, live sports, original content.

The Head of Engineering, **Fatima Ould**, is building out their infrastructure team.
"We have a CDN problem," she says during the intro call. "And a storage problem.
And if I'm being honest, a notification problem. Can you start Monday?"

You can.
