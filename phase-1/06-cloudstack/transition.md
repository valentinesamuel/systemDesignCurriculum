# Leaving CloudStack

A year. You nearly stayed longer — the multi-tenancy problems are deep and endlessly
interesting, and Seo-yeon is one of the best engineering leaders you've worked for.

The fourth noisy-neighbor incident was the last one. The database governance system
went live six weeks after your design. Zero noisy-neighbor incidents in the following
nine months. When Nexus Financial's contract came up for renewal, they signed — and
expanded to the enterprise tier. The auth system handled their Okta integration
without a hitch.

CloudStack grew from 3,200 to 4,800 tenants during your time. The billing system
audit found $127,000 in overcharges across 23 tenants from the triple-aggregation
bug. CloudStack refunded every dollar and added a $500 credit as goodwill. Most
of the affected tenants stayed.

Seo-yeon offers you a senior engineering lead role. You turn it down — not because
you don't want it, but because you feel like there's a gap in your technical
knowledge you need to fill. You've built systems that serve users. You haven't
built a system that helps users *find* things. Search. Full-text. Elasticsearch.

**PulseCommerce** is a Series C e-commerce platform — think a B2B-focused Shopify —
building infrastructure for mid-market retailers. They have a search problem.
150 million product SKUs across 8,000 merchants, and their search results are
considered, charitably, "not very good."

The Head of Engineering, **Nnamdi Okafor**, is direct on the intro call: "Our
merchants are losing customers to Amazon because Amazon's search finds the product
and ours doesn't. We need to fix this. We need someone who understands search
at the systems level."

You understand systems. You'll learn search.
