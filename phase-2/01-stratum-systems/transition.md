# Stratum Systems — Transition

Eight months at Stratum. The distributed tracing system went live three weeks before the Werner Hoffmann deadline. The first trace through the full 14-DC clearance pipeline showed a 4.7-second delay at the Lisbon DC customs gateway — a misconfigured connection pool that had been silently slowing clearances for 11 months. Werner Hoffmann sent a one-sentence email: "Thank you."

The NERC CIP audit metaphor would come later — at Stratum, the equivalent was the Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank go-live. All 14 DCs operational, all 7 regulatory data residency requirements satisfied, zero customs rejections in the first 30 days. Klaus Reinholt presented at a Hamburg logistics conference and mentioned "our distributed architecture team" without naming names. Priya forwarded the conference summary with a single word: "Earned."

Your exit performance review: "Built three systems in eight months that should have taken three engineers three years. Leave date negotiated to January 15th."

On your last day, a Slack DM from Marcus Webb: "Priya told me about the WAL fix. Amara doesn't give compliments. The fact that she sent that message to the all-hands channel means something. Clean handover documentation. The next person deserves the same runway you had."

The next morning, a recruiter from NexaCare — a pharmaceutical clinical trials platform — reaches out. They're building the "Stripe of clinical data" and they need someone who understands distributed systems at scale. The Stratum work has apparently become known in certain circles.

You're moving to pharma. The data is more sensitive. The stakes are different. But the distributed systems problems are, in some ways, exactly the same.
