# GigGrid — Company Introduction

GigGrid is the infrastructure layer for the gig economy — the platform that enterprise clients (hospitals, retailers, logistics companies) and staffing agencies use to dispatch and manage on-demand workers. Eight million registered workers. Two million active on any given day. Thirty countries. Every time a hospital needs a shift nurse at 6AM or a warehouse needs 200 pickers for a Black Friday surge, GigGrid's platform is the thing that finds them, notifies them, and assigns the job.

The platform's tagline is "workforce infrastructure." The truth is closer to "an extremely distributed real-time matching problem with compliance requirements in 30 jurisdictions."

**Amara Chen** — who you remember as the meticulous DBA at Stratum Systems — has become CTO. The transformation is visible immediately: she's traded her monitor-covered desk for a standing desk with a single large display, and her Slack messages are now blunt executive summaries instead of cryptic database diagnostics. But her instinct for precision hasn't changed.

You join as **Principal Engineer** after TradeSpark. Your offer letter arrived before you finished reading Amara's LinkedIn message.

First day: the job dispatch SLA dashboard is entirely red. Every metric. Amara meets you at the door.

**Amara**: "I've been managing this by putting out fires. I need someone who can burn down the architecture and rebuild it. That's you. Where do you want to start?"

You look at the dashboard. You start with the obvious question: "What's actually breaking?"

**Amara**: "Everything. But start with why the same job is getting assigned to two workers simultaneously. That one has legal implications."
