# GigGrid — Transition

Eight months at GigGrid. During the Series C raise, one of the investors specifically mentioned "platform reliability improvements in 2025" in their due diligence note. The deployment incident rate dropped from 3 per quarter to 0. The double-assignment problem — the one that had legal teams involved — dropped to zero in month three.

Amara's exit interview comment was characteristically concise: "I hired you to rebuild the infrastructure. You rebuilt it and documented it so well that a junior engineer could maintain it. That's rare."

The job dispatch SLA dashboard that was entirely red on your first day: entirely green for the last 4 months.

The stock options from Series C vest on a 3-year cliff. You leave them behind.

Your last week, Kenji Mori — who's been in enterprise sales the whole time you've been there — sends a LinkedIn message you don't expect: "I don't usually thank engineers. I'm in sales. But the search redesign you shipped in month three increased job acceptance rates by 34%. That's measurable revenue. Thank you."

You send one back: "Thank you for the worker quotes. They were better requirements than any spec I've ever seen."

The following Monday, a Slack notification from an external workspace: an invitation from **Apex Financial Systems**. The message is brief: "We'd like to discuss a Principal Systems Architect role. The project is complex. The brief is attached."

You read the brief. It's four sentences long and deliberately ambiguous. You recognize the shape of it.

This feels like an interview.
