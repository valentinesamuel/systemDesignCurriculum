# VenueFlow — Company Introduction

## The Infrastructure Layer Nobody Sees

VenueFlow is not a ticketing company. That distinction matters to everyone who works here, and they will correct you within twenty minutes of your first meeting. VenueFlow is the infrastructure layer beneath ticketing — the platform that 12,000 venues across 35 countries rely on to sell 50 million tickets a year. VenueFlow powers the seat maps, inventory engines, real-time availability APIs, and fan-facing checkout flows that dozens of ticketing brands white-label and resell. When a ticket doesn't sell, nobody blames VenueFlow. When 180,000 fans see an error page in four minutes, the ticketing brand's Twitter mentions explode — but the engineers who know, know it was VenueFlow's routing layer.

You join as Principal Architect three weeks after wrapping your last engagement at Crestline. The recruiter called it "a once-in-a-decade infrastructure challenge." You assumed that was recruiter language. It wasn't.

CTO Zara Ahmed meets you in the lobby on your first day. Former Staff Engineer at Ticketmaster. Two patents on distributed seat locking. Exactly the kind of person who will not be impressed by your résumé. She shakes your hand, hands you a coffee, and walks you directly to a conference room. On the table is a printout of a Twitter thread. She smooths it flat with one palm.

"That was us," she says, tapping a screenshot of an error page — the kind with a sad cartoon mascot and a vague apology. The tweet has 2.1 million impressions. The artist's name in the hashtag is Taylor Swift. "One point eight million error pages in four minutes. We processed about eleven percent of the demand we expected. The other eighty-nine percent got a 503 and a form email."

She sits down across from you. "I came here eighteen months ago to fix this. I've made progress. But I need someone who can look at the whole system — not just the ticketing layer, not just the queue, the whole thing — and tell me what we're still missing."

She slides a second sheet across the table. It's a hand-drawn architecture diagram, dense with arrows. "Let's talk about how you'd fix it."
