# Transition: IronWatch → CarbonLedger

You can't tell anyone what you worked on at IronWatch. That's not a figure of speech — it's a clause in the NDA, and the CDA (Classified Disclosure Agreement) beneath it, and possibly the third document you signed that had no title at the top. You pack up your laminated SCIF badge and leave it at the security desk. Tuck shakes your hand and says, "The IG review went well. You should feel good about that," which is the most anyone at IronWatch will ever say about what happened there. On the drive home, you think about Carolyn Marsh's acquisition question, about the data diode's physics-enforced one-way guarantee, about Thomas Ng asking "how do you define tamper-evident?" — and you realize that the entire engagement was, at its core, about one problem: what happens to a system when the humans running it cannot be trusted? The answer you built, in hardware and cryptographic chains and WORM tape, was: you take the humans out of the loop wherever possible, and you make the loop's history permanently readable. Your next engagement is a climate tech startup called CarbonLedger, which sounds approximately as far from classified threat intelligence as it is possible to get — and yet, arriving at their open-plan office with its standing desks and matcha bar, you find on your desk a printed incident report: the carbon credit ledger has a consistency bug that may have let the same credit be sold twice. It's a completely different domain. The fundamental question is identical.
