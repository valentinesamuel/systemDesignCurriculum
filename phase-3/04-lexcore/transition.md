# Transition: Leaving LexCore

The RFC that got rejected in your first month at LexCore — the one proposing a unified document graph model that would have required rewriting the ingestion layer — was accepted eight weeks later, after you rewrote it. Not the technical content. The framing. The first version explained what the system would do. The second version explained what it would cost the business to not do it, and named every team whose quarterly roadmap would be simplified if the graph model existed. You learned something that no system design exercise had ever taught you: at the Staff level, the RFC is not a technical document. It is a political document that happens to contain technical content. The engineering is the easy part. The hard part is making the people who don't care about the engineering care about the outcome.

You spend your last week at LexCore writing a transition doc for whoever inherits the privilege log system. You title it "What the model doesn't know" — a list of every edge case the ML classifier will confidently mishandle, written as a gift for the next engineer who gets a panicked call at 11pm. Then you close your laptop, take a weekend off for the first time in four months, and accept the offer from NeuralBridge, a neuroscience tech company building the data infrastructure layer for next-generation brain-computer interface research. It is, without question, the strangest job you have ever taken. You report your first day on a Monday.
