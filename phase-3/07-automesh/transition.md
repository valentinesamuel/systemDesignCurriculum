The AutoMesh engagement ends on a Friday, over sandwiches in the small kitchen on the third floor. Hiroshi Tanaka brings his own hot sauce because he has been bringing his own hot sauce to every team lunch for twelve years and no office has ever stocked the right kind. Elena Rodriguez gives a short, genuinely warm speech that ends with: "You made us take the FMEA seriously. I hated every minute of it. I'm grateful for every minute of it." Dmitri Volkov — who spent most of the engagement being defended against, interrogated, or having cost lines circled in red — shakes your hand with both of his and hands you a signed letter of recommendation on AutoMesh letterhead, which he folded three times into his jacket pocket before pulling it out as if he had been carrying it for a week, which he probably had.

The drive home is long enough to think. Somewhere on Highway 101, an AutoMesh test vehicle merges two lanes over — white, unmarked except for the sensor pod on the roof, the lidar unit spinning in slow, indifferent circles. You watch it for a moment before the traffic separates you. Five months ago that car was a novelty. Now it is a system you understand down to its message broker replication topology and its OTA bootloader partition scheme and the precise failure mode that could, under the wrong combination of conditions, turn a construction zone into a collision. The gap between "a car that drives itself" and "a car that drives itself safely" is not a gap you can see from the outside. It lives in the architecture. You have spent five months inside that gap, and what you learned is that safety is not a property you add to a system — it is a constraint you design from, from the very first schema column, from the very first fallback behavior, from the very first time someone asks "what happens if this fails?" and you answer honestly instead of optimistically. Your next engagement is already in your inbox: a pharmaceutical data platform in New Jersey, subject line reading "Compliance Architecture — Urgent." You think about what Hiroshi said on the first day: "Don't go easy on us." You write that down, for yourself, before you forget it.
