# Leaving ForgeSense

Six months at ForgeSense taught you what "legacy" really means — not old code, but accumulated trust. The SCADA system running Windows XP in Warsaw wasn't technical debt; it was 15 years of institutional knowledge encoded in a platform nobody documented because nobody expected it to outlive its authors. You inherited a security incident and a compliance gap simultaneously, and you learned that on the factory floor, software bugs have physical consequences. The bidirectional control architecture you designed is now coordinating welding operations on four Volkswagen production lines. When the latency SLA for a safety-critical command is 100 milliseconds, "good enough" is not an engineering standard — it's a liability. The data mesh you built for the twelve factories will take another year to fully adopt, and that's fine: data governance is a slow institutional change, not a deployment. Helena gives you a letter of commendation. Benedikt shakes your hand and says "sorry about the ambush." Rafal says nothing, but when you clear your desk, there is a handwritten sticky note on your monitor: "Ask the right questions. You did."

Next: DeepOcean, where you will discover that "offline-first" is not a feature flag.
