Priya takes you to a rooftop bar in SoMa the week after the postmortem closes, the kind of place with low tables and heat lamps that hum just loud enough to feel like white noise. The sun is going down over the bay, and from up here the water breaks the light into irregular polygons — deep green, copper, gray — that look, if you squint, exactly like a satellite image of a field partitioned for crop rotation. She hands you a glass without asking what you want and says she never thought a Staff Engineer could actually care this deeply about soil moisture, and you tell her that every system, no matter how abstract the architecture diagram looks on a whiteboard, is ultimately connected to something physical — a tractor, a patient, a parcel moving through a warehouse, a family farm that needs to know where to plant the soybeans. She thinks about that for a moment and nods, the way people nod when they're filing something away rather than agreeing. The hardest bugs, you say, are always the ones closest to the ground — the ones where the failure mode is not a stack trace but a farmer wondering why his field is getting fertilized at medium rate everywhere, or forty people who grew something real and won't get paid on time. Priya looks out at the water. She tells you the next season starts in September and you are welcome back. You tell her you'll be watching the carbon credit dashboard from wherever you land next. She laughs and says she'll make sure the alerts actually fire this time.
