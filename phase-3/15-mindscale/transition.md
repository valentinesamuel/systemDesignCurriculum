Your last day at MindScale is a Friday. There's no goodbye party — Dr. Wells had asked you, weeks earlier, whether you wanted one, and you'd said no. You spend the morning doing knowledge transfer with Yusuf: walking him through the purpose-tagging middleware, the deletion pipeline, the consent gate you built for 42 CFR Part 2. He nods at each piece, asks one sharp question per system, and you know it's in good hands. At 4 PM you hand in your badge and Dr. Samara Wells walks you to the elevator herself. She is carrying a coffee mug she's had since before you arrived — it says "DO NO HARM" in faded block letters, and you have always wondered if she means it ironically or not. In the elevator lobby she pauses, and for a moment you think she's going to give you advice, or a reference letter talking point, or a list of what still needs doing. Instead she says: "Most engineers optimize for scale. You optimized for dignity. That's rarer." The elevator opens. You step inside. She doesn't wave. The doors close. Outside it's raining — a thin, steady March rain, not dramatic, just persistent. You stand on the sidewalk for a moment before opening your umbrella. The city moves around you. You think about what she said. You let the rain be the rain.
