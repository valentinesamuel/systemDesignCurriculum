The team lunch is at a Peruvian place two blocks from the office, the kind of restaurant Sofia has been suggesting for months and no one ever had time for. She gives a toast that is characteristically short — "You made the grid harder to break and easier to understand, and those are not the same problem" — and then sits down and lets the conversation drift into the comfortable noise of people who have been through something together. Kwabena, joining by video from Accra, holds up a small package to his laptop camera at the end of the meal: a copy of *Power System Dynamics and Stability* by Jan Machowski, third edition, the spine so thoroughly cracked it opens on its own to Chapter 9. The annotations are in three colors — black for the original reading, red for the year he spent on a SCADA retrofit in Lagos, and green for everything he learned at GlacierGrid, which fills the margins of the last four chapters so densely that the green nearly obscures the printed text. On the inside cover, in careful block letters: *For the next one who has to explain frequency regulation at 3am.* You accept it on the video call and then physically at the office the next morning when the package arrives by courier, and you hold it for a long moment before setting it on your desk beside your laptop. You've been in this industry long enough that you own your own annotated books now — your own copies of things with dog-eared pages and margin notes in two or three colors, your own working theories written in the white space between other people's theorems. You are no longer the person receiving the inheritance. You are the person beginning to leave one.
